Mutation, Variation & Comparisons — Notes

Concise points for 9th grade memorization.

Summary of Mutation Theory

Proposed by Hugo de Vries. Mutations are sudden heritable changes in genes or chromosomes. They can be beneficial, neutral, or harmful. Mutations are the main source of variation, and favorable mutations may lead to evolution. Types include point mutations and chromosomal mutations.

Continuous and Discontinuous Variation

Continuous Variation: Gradual variation in traits; many intermediates exist. Example: height, skin color.

Discontinuous Variation: Distinct categories with no intermediates. Example: blood groups, tongue rolling.

Short notes

  • Struggle for existence: competition among individuals for limited resources.
  • Enormous fertility: organisms produce more offspring than survive, leading to selection.
  • Specialization: adaptation of organs for specific functions enhances survival.
  • Mutation: source of sudden variation; may be inherited.
  • Beneficial mutation: increases chances of survival and reproduction.
  • Variation: differences among individuals; essential for natural selection.

Differentiate between

  • Homologous vs Analogous: Homologous organs — same structure, different function; Analogous — different structure, same function.
  • Lamarckism vs Darwinism: Lamarck — acquired traits inherited; Darwin — natural selection acts on existing variation.
  • Heredity vs Variation: Heredity — transmission of traits; Variation — differences among individuals.
  • Continuous vs Discontinuous: Continuous — range of forms; Discontinuous — distinct categories.
  • Acquired vs Hereditary: Acquired — gained in life, not inherited; Hereditary — passed to offspring.
  • Homologous vs Vestigial: Homologous — structural similarity indicating common ancestry; Vestigial — reduced or non-functional organs.

Give reasons

  • Lamarckism was rejected after Darwinism — lacked evidence for inheritance of acquired traits; Darwinism explained evolution via natural selection.
  • Six-fingered offspring may form from normal parents — mutation can create new traits.
  • Platypus is a bridge animal — has mammalian features (milk) and reptilian/avian features (egg laying, bill).
  • Embryonic similarity (humans, birds, fish) — Haeckel: ontogeny repeats phylogeny; embryos reflect evolutionary history.
  • Tail and ear muscles in humans — vestigial organs, reduced due to lack of function.
