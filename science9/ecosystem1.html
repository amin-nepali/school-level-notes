  • Ecosystem is defined as a community of living (biotic) organisms interacting with their non-living (abiotic) environment in a specific area.
  • The components of an ecosystem are given below:
    • Biotic components: Living organisms (plants, animals, bacteria, fungi)
    • Abiotic components: Non-living factors (temperature, water, light, soil, air)
  • Decomposers are known as organisms (mainly bacteria and fungi) that break down dead matter and recycle nutrients.
  • Examples of top consumers in a pond are given below:
    • Large carnivorous fish (e.g., Pike)
    • Fish-eating birds (e.g., Heron)
  • Abiotic components in an ecosystem are called non-living factors; examples are temperature and water.
  • Examples of decomposers in a grassland are given below:
    • Bacteria
    • Fungi
  • The type of interrelation between sea sponges and corals is known as mutualism (both benefit).
  • The role of algae in a pond ecosystem is defined as primary producers making food through photosynthesis at the base of the food chain.
  • The role of bacteria in a grassland ecosystem is given below:
    • Act as decomposers
    • Perform nitrogen fixation (convert air nitrogen into usable forms)
  • Ecological interaction is defined as the relationship or interaction that occurs between organisms in an ecosystem (can be beneficial, harmful, or neutral).
  • The type of interaction found between goats struggling for grass is called competition (both harmed for limited resources).

üìñ Long Answer Type Questions (Definition Style)

a. Biotic & Abiotic Components

  • Biotic components are defined as living organisms in an ecosystem (producers, consumers, decomposers).
  • Abiotic components are defined as non-living factors in an ecosystem (temperature, water, light, soil, air).

b. Green Plants as Producers

  • Green plants are called producers because they make their own food using chlorophyll, carbon dioxide, water, and sunlight.
  • They are the starting point of energy in all food chains.

c. Key Terms

Term Definition
ProducerDefined as an organism that makes its own food (e.g., plants)
EcosystemKnown as a community of living and non-living factors interacting
DecomposerOrganism that breaks down dead matter (e.g., fungi)
Food webCalled a network of interconnected food chains
ParasitismDefined as a relationship where one species benefits and the other is harmed (e.g., tapeworm)
SymbiosisKnown as a close long-term relationship between two different species
CommensalismDefined as a relationship where one species benefits and the other is neither helped nor harmed
Trophic levelDefined as the position of an organism in a food chain

d. Increased Tertiary Consumers

  • Increased tertiary consumers cause over-predation on secondary consumers, so their population decreases.
  • Primary consumer (herbivore) population increases due to fewer secondary consumers.
  • Over-grazing by herbivores decreases producer (plant) population ‚Üí ecosystem imbalance.

e. Abiotic Factors

  • Abiotic factors affecting the environment are given below:
    • Light (Solar Energy)
    • Temperature
    • Water
    • Soil
  • Temperature is defined as crucial because it controls metabolic rate and enzyme function; life exists within specific temperature ranges, extremes limit species distribution.

f. Food Chain

Grass ‚Üí Grasshopper ‚Üí Frog ‚Üí Snake

Food chain is defined as a linear transfer of energy from one organism to another. Energy is lost at each trophic level.

g. Grassland Ecosystem

  • Grassland ecosystem is defined as an ecosystem dominated by grasses with few trees, found in areas with moderate rainfall.
  • Producers: Grasses
  • Consumers: Grazing animals (zebra, deer) and carnivores (lion)
  • Abiotic: Moderate rainfall, fertile soil, common seasonal fires

h. Impacts of Climate Change

  • Loss of biodiversity: Habitats like coral reefs are destroyed (bleaching)
  • Species shift: Organisms migrate to find suitable temperatures
  • Disrupted cycles: Timing of plant flowering and animal migration affected
  • Extreme events: Droughts, floods, wildfires destroy habitats

i. Migration Effects

  • Source ecosystem: Less grazing pressure on plants; temporary food shortage for predators
  • Destination ecosystem: More grazing pressure; temporary food for predators; adds nutrients via waste
  • Migration helps maintain seasonal energy redistribution and ecosystem balance
