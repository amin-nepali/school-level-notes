Mushroom — Short & Long Answer Notes (Grade 9)

Concise, punctuated points + simple diagrams for easy memorization.

Short Answer Type Questions

a. What is mushroom?
• A mushroom is the edible or visible fruiting body of some fungi (mainly basidiomycetes). It develops from the mycelium to produce and release spores.
b. Where are mushrooms grown?
• Mushrooms grow on moist, shaded places rich in organic matter — e.g., decaying logs, compost, leaf litter, soil rich in humus, and specially prepared beds (straw, sawdust, or compost) in cultivation.
c. Which type of wild mushrooms are edible?
• Common edible wild types: many Agaricus (button types), some Boletes (with pores), morels (Morchella), and puffballs — but correct identification by an expert is essential.
d. What are the different parts of a mushroom?
• Pileus (cap), Gills (lamellae) or pores, Stipe (stem), Annulus (ring), Volva (cup at base), and Mycelium (root-like threads).
e. What is hypha?
• Hypha (plural hyphae) is a single thread-like filament of fungal tissue; many hyphae together form the mycelium.
f. What are basidiospores?
• Basidiospores are sexual spores produced on a basidium (club-shaped cell) found on the gills of basidiomycete mushrooms; they disperse to form new mycelia.
g. Name any three edible mushrooms.
• Button mushroom (Agaricus bisporus), Oyster mushroom (Pleurotus ostreatus), Shiitake (Lentinula edodes).
h. Name any two poisonous mushrooms.
• Death cap (Amanita phalloides), Destroying angel (Amanita virosa). (Highly toxic — avoid.)
i. What is rhizomorphs?
• Rhizomorphs are thick, root-like bundles of hyphae that transport nutrients and water across distances and help the fungus colonize new areas.
j. What type of wild variety of mushrooms are poisonous?
• Many wild Amanita species, some Galerina, some Inocybe and certain false morels are poisonous — varieties with white gills, a ring, and a volva are especially suspect.
k. What is annulus?
• Annulus is the ring on the stipe left from the partial veil; it may be present, partial, or absent depending on the species.
l. Write the function of the pileus.
• The pileus (cap) protects the spore-bearing surface (gills or pores) and helps spread spores by providing a surface for basidia and directing air flow for spore release.
m. What is the function of the gills in mushrooms?
• Gills (lamellae) increase surface area to hold many basidia where basidiospores form and are released for dispersal.

Long Answer Type Questions

a. Health benefits of mushrooms (any four)

  • Rich source of nutrients — vitamins (B-complex, D), minerals (selenium, potassium), and protein — help overall nutrition.
  • Low in calories and fat — good for weight management and healthy diets.
  • Contain antioxidants and beta-glucans — support immunity and may help lower cholesterol.
  • Good source of dietary fiber and prebiotics — support digestion and gut health.

b. Structure of the basidiocarp (with labelled diagram)

Basidiocarp (mushroom body) parts — short notes:

  • Pileus (cap): umbrella-like top that protects the spore-bearing surface.
  • Gills (lamellae): thin plates under the cap where basidia form.
  • Stipe (stem): supports the cap and lifts gills for better spore dispersal.
  • Annulus (ring): remnant of a partial veil on the stipe.
  • Volva: cup-like structure at base — remnant of universal veil in some species.
  • Mycelium: network of hyphae in substrate — food-absorbing body.

c. Life cycle of a mushroom (brief explanation + diagram)

Simple steps — memorize in order:

  1. Basidiospores released from basidia on gills.
  2. Spores germinate → form primary (monokaryotic) mycelium (single nucleus per cell).
  3. Two compatible primary mycelia meet → plasmogamy → form secondary (dikaryotic) mycelium.
  4. Secondary mycelium grows, forms a basidiocarp (mushroom) under suitable conditions.
  5. In basidia of gills, karyogamy and meiosis occur → basidiospores formed → cycle repeats.

d. Small scale cultivation of mushroom (process)

  1. Prepare spawn: grow mushroom mycelium on sterilized grains (rice/wheat).
  2. Prepare substrate: straw, sawdust, or compost — pasteurize/sterilize to remove competitors.
  3. Mix spawn into cooled substrate in clean trays or bags (spawn run).
  4. Incubate in dark, warm conditions until mycelium fully colonizes substrate.
  5. Initiate fruiting: lower temperature, increase fresh air and light, keep humidity high (80–90%).
  6. Harvest mature mushrooms by cutting at the base; repeat for multiple flushes.

e. How to store mushrooms for a long time

Best methods:

  • Refrigeration: keep fresh mushrooms in paper bag in fridge 3–7 days (short term).
  • Drying: slice and sun-dry or use a dehydrator → store in airtight containers (months to year).
  • Freezing: blanch or sauté then freeze in sealed bags — good for cooked mushrooms (months).
  • Canning or pickling: pressure canning for long shelf life; pickling for flavour and storage.

f. Five advantages of mushroom cultivation (detailed)

  1. Quick yield: Many edible mushrooms (e.g., oyster) give harvests within weeks—good for small farmers and entrepreneurs.
  2. High nutrition: Mushrooms provide proteins, vitamins and minerals, improving local food security and nutrition.
  3. Low space requirement: Can be grown indoors on shelves or in small rooms — suitable for urban farming.
  4. Utilizes waste: Substrates like straw, sawdust, or agricultural waste are reused — reduces waste and cost.
  5. Income source: High market demand and short cycles allow regular income with low capital investment.

g. Characteristics of poisonous mushrooms

  • Often have white gills, a cup-like volva at base, and an annulus — but many exceptions exist; identification by features alone is risky.
  • Some cause delayed severe liver/kidney damage (e.g., Amanita phalloides) — toxins not destroyed by cooking.
  • Odour or taste is not a reliable indicator; some deadly species smell pleasant.
  • Colourful or unusual shapes do not always mean poisonous — many edible species are colourful too.

h. Nutrients found in mushrooms (some important ones)

Protein, B vitamins (B1, B2, B3, B5), vitamin D (especially when exposed to sunlight), potassium, phosphorus, selenium, dietary fiber, and antioxidants (ergothioneine).

i. Why mushrooms are popular for food value

  • Good nutrition: vitamins, minerals, and protein with low calories.
  • Versatile: used in soups, curries, stir-fries, and snacks — suits many cuisines.
  • Meaty texture: used as meat substitute in vegetarian dishes.
  • Easy to grow and available year-round via cultivation.

j. How to make people aware that most wild mushrooms are unsafe

  • Educate: run short workshops and posters showing dangerous traits and safe alternatives.
  • Use simple rules: "If unsure — do not eat" and "Never eat wild mushrooms without expert identification".
  • Share local guides: pictures of common edible vs. poisonous species in local language.
  • Encourage cultivation: promote home-grown edible mushrooms as safer options.

4. Differentiate Between (short points)

a. Hypha vs MyceliumHypha: single filament of fungal tissue. • Mycelium: network of many hyphae forming the main vegetative body.
b. Poisonous vs Edible mushroomsPoisonous: contain toxins; may cause illness or death; often require expert ID. • Edible: safe to eat, nutritious, generally cultivated or confidently identified.
c. Primary vs Secondary myceliaPrimary: monokaryotic, from germinated spores. • Secondary: dikaryotic, formed after fusion (plasmogamy) of compatible primary mycelia and able to form fruiting bodies.
d. Paraphyses vs BasidiumParaphyses: sterile, hair-like supporting cells in hymenium. • Basidium: fertile club-shaped cell that produces basidiospores.

5. Give Reasons (short answers)

  1. Mushroom is a saprophytic fungus: it feeds on dead organic matter (decaying wood, leaves), helping in decomposition and nutrient cycling.
  2. Basidium is the fertile cell: basidium undergoes karyogamy and meiosis to produce basidiospores — it is the spore-producing (fertile) cell.
  3. Some mushrooms important for mankind: they provide food, medicine (antibiotics, immune-supporting compounds), and income through cultivation.
  4. Mushrooms are decomposers ecologically: they break down complex organic materials (cellulose, lignin) into simpler compounds used by plants and other organisms.
  5. Cultivated mushrooms are more reliable than wild mushrooms: cultivation uses known safe strains, controlled substrate and conditions — reduces risk of poisonous look-alikes and contamination.
