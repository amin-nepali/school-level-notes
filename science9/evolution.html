Evolution — Short & Long Answer Notes

Concise, punctuated points for quick memorization — suitable for a 9th grade student.

2. Short answer type questions — Punctuated notes

a. Define organic evolution.

Change in the heritable characteristics of populations of organisms over successive generations; gradual modification that leads to new species.

b. What are fossils?

Remains, impressions or traces of organisms preserved in rock or sediments; record past life and environments.

c. Define embryo.

Early developmental stage of an organism after fertilization; basic body plan and organs begin to form.

d. Define palaeontological evidence.

Evidence of evolution derived from fossils and the fossil record; shows changes in organisms through geological time.

e. Define homologous organs.

Structures having similar origin and basic anatomy but different functions; indicate common ancestry. (e.g., forelimbs of human, bat, whale.)

f. What are analogous organs?

Structures with similar function but different origin and structure; result of convergent evolution. (e.g., wings of insects and birds.)

g. What are mutagens?

Physical or chemical agents that cause changes (mutations) in DNA; examples: radiation, certain chemicals, some viruses.

h. Give any two examples of vestigial organs in human.

Appendix; wisdom teeth (third molars). — (also: coccyx, muscles for ear movement).

i. What is the sudden chromosomal change in organisms called?

Chromosomal mutation — a sudden change in chromosome structure or number (e.g., deletion, duplication, translocation, polyploidy).

j. Who suggested natural selection as the main cause of evolution?

Charles Darwin.

k. Who suggested use and disuse of organs as the main cause of evolution?

Jean-Baptiste Lamarck.

l. Who suggested mutation theory?

Hugo de Vries.

m. Mention any two types of mutation.

Point mutation; chromosomal mutation.

n. Define continuous variation.

Variation in a trait that shows a range and grade of forms (e.g., height, skin color); many intermediate types; usually polygenic and influenced by environment.

o. Define discontinuous variation.

Variation where individuals fall into distinct categories with no intermediates (e.g., blood groups, presence or absence of a feature); usually controlled by single genes.

3. Long answer type questions — Short, memorable paragraphs & points

a. Mention any four evidences of organic evolution.

1. Fossil record: shows succession and transitional forms; layered fossils indicate change through geological time.
2. Comparative anatomy: homologous organs suggest common ancestry; vestigial organs indicate loss of former function.
3. Embryology: early embryos of different animals show similar stages — common developmental patterns.
4. Biochemistry & genetics: similarity in DNA, proteins and metabolic pathways among species indicates shared heritage.

b. How do homologous and analogous organs give evidence of evolution?

• Homologous organs: similar structure and origin but different functions — indicate divergence from a common ancestor (e.g., forelimbs of vertebrates).
• Analogous organs: similar functions but different structures and origins — indicate convergent evolution where unrelated organisms adapt to similar environments (e.g., wings for flying in birds and insects).
→ Together: homologous organs support common descent; analogous organs show adaptation by natural selection to similar problems.

c. What are vestigial organs? How are they different from homologous organs?

• Vestigial organs: reduced or non-functional structures that were functional in ancestors (e.g., human appendix, pelvic bones in whales).
• Difference from homologous organs: homologous organs emphasize common structural origin despite differing functions; vestigial organs emphasize loss or reduction of function over time.
→ A vestigial organ can be homologous to a functional organ in an ancestor (so categories can overlap).

d. Write in short about connecting links and embryonic evidence.

• Connecting links (transitional forms): fossils or living species that show intermediate features between two groups (e.g., Archaeopteryx — reptile + bird features).
• Embryonic evidence: embryos of diverse animals show similar early stages (e.g., pharyngeal pouches in vertebrate embryos) — suggests common ancestry and conserved developmental genes.

e. Write in short about Lamarckism, natural selection and variation.

• Lamarckism: theory by Lamarck — use and disuse of organs and inheritance of acquired traits drive evolution; organisms actively adapt during their lifetime.
• Natural selection (Darwin): individuals vary; more offspring are produced than survive; those with favorable traits survive and reproduce — traits become more common.
• Variation: natural differences among individuals (heritable + non-heritable); variation is the raw material on which natural selection acts.

f. What are the drawbacks of Lamarckism and Darwinism?

• Lamarckism drawbacks: lacks evidence for inheritance of acquired characters; no mechanism to explain how acquired changes are passed to offspring.
• Darwinism drawbacks (as originally proposed): did not explain the source of variation or the mechanism of inheritance (Mendelian genetics and mutation theory solved these later); gradualism may not explain sudden large changes.

g. What is natural selection? How does it lead to evolution?

• Definition: process where individuals with advantageous heritable traits survive and reproduce more successfully than others, changing the population over generations.
• How it leads to evolution:

  • Variation exists among individuals.
  • Environmental pressures create a struggle for resources.
  • Individuals with favorable traits survive and reproduce.
  • Favorable traits increase in frequency over generations.
  • Accumulation of changes may produce new species (speciation).

h. Mention the major points of Darwinism.

1. Variation: individuals in a species show variation.
2. Overproduction: organisms produce more offspring than can survive.
3. Struggle for existence: competition for limited resources.
4. Natural selection: differential survival and reproduction due to favorable traits.
5. Survival of the fittest: those best adapted leave more offspring.
6. Gradualism & common descent: species change slowly and share common ancestors.
