3h. How do fungi digest their food?
Fungi use external digestion to get their food. They release special enzymes outside their body onto the food material. These enzymes break down complex nutrients into simpler forms. Then the fungi absorb these digested nutrients through their cell walls. This is called saprophytic nutrition. Fungi cannot make their own food like plants, so they depend on dead organic matter or living organisms for nutrition.
3i. Enlist the characteristics of the kingdom Monera.
Kingdom Monera includes all prokaryotic organisms like bacteria. They are unicellular and very small in size. They lack a true nucleus and membrane-bound organelles. Their genetic material (DNA) is not enclosed in a nuclear membrane. They can be autotrophic (make own food) or heterotrophic (depend on others). Their cell wall is made of peptidoglycan. They reproduce asexually by binary fission. Examples include bacteria, blue-green algae, and mycoplasma.
3j. Why is it necessary to classify living organisms? Give reasons.
Classification makes studying millions of organisms easier and more organized. It helps us understand the relationships between different species and how they evolved. We can identify organisms quickly by looking at their group characteristics. It helps in discovering new species and understanding biodiversity. Classification also helps in agriculture, medicine, and conservation efforts. Without classification, studying the huge variety of life forms would be impossible and confusing. It provides a universal system that scientists worldwide can use.
3k. What is a scientific name of an organism and how is it written?
A scientific name is the unique biological name given to each organism based on binomial nomenclature. It consists of two parts: the genus name and the species name. The genus name is written first with a capital letter, followed by the species name in lowercase. Both names are written in italics or underlined. For example, humans are named Homo sapiens. This system was developed by Carolus Linnaeus and is used universally by scientists to avoid confusion.
3l. What kinds of organisms are grouped under Protista? Would you consider this kingdom a natural one?
Kingdom Protista includes simple eukaryotic organisms that don't fit in other kingdoms. It contains unicellular organisms like amoeba, paramecium, and euglena. Some protists are autotrophic like algae, while others are heterotrophic like protozoans. This kingdom is not considered natural because organisms here are very different from each other. It's more like a collection of organisms that couldn't be placed elsewhere. They only share the feature of being simple eukaryotes.
3m. Which organisms are called primitive and how are they different from the so-called advanced organisms?
Primitive organisms are those with simple body structure and ancient features, like bacteria and algae. They appeared early in evolution and have basic cellular organization. Advanced organisms like flowering plants and mammals have complex body structures with specialized organs and tissues. Primitive organisms usually reproduce asexually, while advanced ones often reproduce sexually. Advanced organisms show better adaptation to environment and have more efficient systems for nutrition, respiration, and reproduction compared to primitive forms.

4. Differentiate Between:
a) Prokaryotes and Eukaryotes
Prokaryotes: These are simple cells without a true nucleus. Their DNA floats freely in the cell. They lack membrane-bound organelles like mitochondria. They are usually unicellular. Examples include bacteria.

Eukaryotes: These have a well-defined nucleus enclosed by nuclear membrane. They contain membrane-bound organelles. They can be unicellular or multicellular. Examples include plants, animals, and fungi.
b) Genus and Species
Genus: It is a group of related species that share common characteristics. It's a higher taxonomic rank. The genus name is written first with a capital letter. Example: Panthera (includes lions, tigers).

Species: It is the basic unit of classification. Members can interbreed and produce fertile offspring. It's written after genus name in lowercase. Example: Panthera leo (lion).
c) Protista and Monera
Protista: These are eukaryotic organisms with a true nucleus. They are mostly unicellular but some are multicellular. They have membrane-bound organelles. Examples include amoeba, paramecium.

Monera: These are prokaryotic organisms without a true nucleus. They are always unicellular. They lack membrane-bound organelles. Examples include bacteria and blue-green algae.
d) Two Kingdom and Five Kingdom Classifications
Two Kingdom: Organisms were divided into only Plantae and Animalia. It was very simple but not accurate. Bacteria and fungi were wrongly placed in plants. It didn't consider cell structure differences.

Five Kingdom: Organisms are divided into Monera, Protista, Fungi, Plantae, and Animalia. It's more scientific and accurate. It considers cell structure, mode of nutrition, and body organization. It's widely accepted today.
e) Yeast and Mushroom
Yeast: It is a unicellular fungus. It's microscopic in size and can't be seen without a microscope. It reproduces by budding. It's used in baking and brewing industries.

Mushroom: It is a multicellular fungus with a visible fruiting body. It's large enough to be seen easily. It reproduces by spores. It grows on dead organic matter in damp places.
5. Give Reasons:
a) The five-kingdom classification is more advantageous than the two-kingdom classification.
Five-kingdom classification is better because it considers cell structure (prokaryotic vs eukaryotic). It separates fungi from plants based on their mode of nutrition. Bacteria are properly placed in Monera. Protista accommodates simple eukaryotes that don't fit elsewhere. It reflects evolutionary relationships more accurately. The two-kingdom system was too simple and created confusion by grouping very different organisms together.
b) Classification of living beings is essential.
Classification organizes millions of species systematically, making study easier. It helps identify organisms and understand their characteristics quickly. We can predict properties of an organism by knowing its group. It shows evolutionary relationships between species. Classification is important for fields like medicine, agriculture, and conservation. Without it, studying and managing biodiversity would be extremely difficult and chaotic.
c) The protistans are more advanced than the monerans.
Protistans are eukaryotic with a well-defined nucleus, while monerans are prokaryotic. Protists have membrane-bound organelles like mitochondria and chloroplasts, which monerans lack. They have more complex cell organization and life processes. Protists show more diverse modes of nutrition and reproduction. They appeared later in evolution, showing advancement. Their cellular complexity makes them more organized than simple monerans.
d) Mushroom is a saprophytic fungus.
Mushrooms grow on dead and decaying organic matter like fallen logs and leaf litter. They secrete digestive enzymes onto dead material to break it down. They absorb the digested nutrients for their growth. They cannot make their own food through photosynthesis. They play an important role in decomposition and nutrient recycling in ecosystems. This feeding on dead matter makes them saprophytic organisms.
e) We classify organisms.
Classification helps organize the enormous diversity of life on Earth. It makes identifying and studying organisms much easier and faster. We can understand evolutionary relationships and how species are related. Classification helps in research, medicine, agriculture, and conservation work. It provides a universal language for scientists worldwide. Without classification, the study of biology would be disorganized and confusing for everyone.
