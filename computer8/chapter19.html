Answers to Textbook Questions

4. Give Reasons

  1. Ionic compounds have high melting points.

    Because they are formed by the strong electrostatic force of attraction between positively charged ions (cations) and negatively charged ions (anions). A large amount of heat energy is required to overcome this strong force and break the crystal lattice structure, leading to high melting points.

  2. Metals are lustrous.

    Because the free electrons present on the surface of metals absorb energy from incident light and then re-emit it. This gives metals a shining or reflective surface, known as metallic lustre.

  3. Metals are ductile.

    Because of their specific crystalline structure, which allows the layers of atoms to slide over one another when a force is applied (without breaking the metallic bonds). This property, known as ductility, allows them to be drawn into thin wires.

  4. Lead is harmful to humans.

    Because it is a toxic heavy metal that can accumulate in the body (in the bones and soft tissues), causing damage to the nervous system, kidneys, and blood formation. Lead poisoning can lead to serious health issues, especially in children.

  5. Metals are good conductors of electricity.

    Because they possess **mobile (or free) electrons** in their structure. When an electric potential difference is applied, these free electrons can easily move and carry the electric current through the metal.

  6. Metals are used to make cooking utensils.

    Because metals are **good conductors of heat** and have **high melting points**. This allows them to transfer heat efficiently to the food and withstand the high temperatures used for cooking without melting.

  7. Electroplating of copper can be made on iron but not on silver.

    This is because **copper is less reactive than iron** but **more reactive than silver**. For effective and long-lasting electroplating, the metal being deposited (copper) must be less reactive than the base metal (iron) to prevent displacement. Since copper is more reactive than silver, it would not form a stable or effective plating layer on silver under normal conditions.

  8. Sodium is kept immersed in kerosene oil.

    Sodium is a highly reactive metal. It reacts vigorously and exothermically with **oxygen** (in the air) and **water** (including moisture in the air), which can cause a fire or explosion. Kerosene oil is a non-polar hydrocarbon that does not react with sodium and prevents its contact with air and moisture, thus protecting it from reaction.

5. Differentiate Between Metals and Non-metals in Five Points

Property Metals Non-metals
Physical State Usually solids at room temperature (except Mercury, which is a liquid). Exist as solids, liquids, or gases at room temperature (e.g., Carbon (solid), Bromine (liquid), Oxygen (gas)).
Lustre Have a shiny surface, exhibiting **metallic lustre** (e.g., Gold, Silver). Generally dull or non-lustrous (except Iodine and Graphite).
Malleability & Ductility Are **malleable** (can be beaten into thin sheets) and **ductile** (can be drawn into thin wires). Are **brittle** (break easily) and are neither malleable nor ductile.
Conductivity Good conductors of heat and electricity (e.g., Copper, Aluminium). Poor conductors of heat and electricity (except Graphite).
Density & Hardness Generally have high density and are hard (except Sodium, Potassium, which are soft). Generally have low density and are soft (except Diamond, which is the hardest known substance).

2. Short Answer Type Questions

  1. What is metal? Give an example of a shining non-metal and an example of the best conductor of heat.
    • **Metal:** An element that is typically solid, hard, lustrous, malleable, ductile, has a high melting point, and is a good conductor of heat and electricity. They usually form positive ions (cations) by losing electrons.
    • **Shining non-metal:** **Iodine** or **Graphite**.
    • **Best conductor of heat:** **Silver**. (Copper is also an excellent conductor and is more commonly used).
  2. What is the difference between conductor and insulator?
    • **Conductor:** A substance that allows heat or electric current to pass through it easily (e.g., Copper, Aluminium).
    • **Insulator:** A substance that does not allow heat or electric current to pass through it easily (e.g., Wood, Rubber, Plastic).
  3. What is a malleable metal?

    A malleable metal is a metal that can be beaten or hammered into thin sheets without breaking. This property is known as **malleability**.

  4. What is metallic bond?

    A metallic bond is the strong electrostatic force of attraction between the positively charged metal ions (nuclei and inner shell electrons) and the surrounding sea of mobile (delocalised) electrons.

  5. Name any two soft metals.

    **Sodium** ($\text{Na}$) and **Potassium** ($\text{K}$). (They can be easily cut with a knife).

  6. Which non-metal can conduct electricity?

    **Graphite** (an allotrope of Carbon).

  7. What are the metals found as minerals in human body?

    Essential metallic minerals include **Iron ($\text{Fe}$)**, **Calcium ($\text{Ca}$)**, **Sodium ($\text{Na}$)**, **Potassium ($\text{K}$)**, **Magnesium ($\text{Mg}$)**, and **Zinc ($\text{Zn}$)**.

  8. What is metallic lustre?

    Metallic lustre is the characteristic shininess or reflection of light exhibited by metals. It is due to the presence of free electrons on the metal's surface.

  9. Write any four plant sources of iron in human body.

    Spinach, Lentils (Daal), Beans, Tofu, Raisins, and Dried Apricots are good plant sources of iron.

  10. Name two metals which are harmful to human beings.

    **Lead** ($\text{Pb}$) and **Mercury** ($\text{Hg}$). (Cadmium and Arsenic compounds are also harmful).

  2. Give an example of metal which:
    • i) **is a liquid at room temperature:** **Mercury** ($\text{Hg}$)
    • ii) **is a poor conductor of electricity:** **Lead** ($\text{Pb}$)
    • iii) **can be easily cut with a knife:** **Sodium** ($\text{Na}$) or **Potassium** ($\text{K}$)
  3. What would you observe when zinc is added to a dilute hydrochloric acid? Write the chemical reaction that takes place.
    • **Observation:** You would observe the rapid formation of **bubbles** or **effervescence** (indicating the release of a gas) and the zinc metal will dissolve. The gas released is **hydrogen** ($\text{H}_2$), which burns with a 'pop' sound when tested with a burning splint.
    • **Chemical Reaction:** Zinc metal reacts with dilute hydrochloric acid to form zinc chloride and hydrogen gas.
    • $$ \text{Zn}(s) + 2\text{HCl}(aq) \longrightarrow \text{ZnCl}_2(aq) + \text{H}_2(g) $$
  4. Write the equation for the reaction of:
    • i) **calcium and water:** Calcium reacts with water to form calcium hydroxide and hydrogen gas. $$ \text{Ca}(s) + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}(l) \longrightarrow \text{Ca}(\text{OH})_2(aq) + \text{H}_2(g) $$
    • ii) **sodium and oxygen:** Sodium reacts with oxygen to form sodium oxide. $$ 4\text{Na}(s) + \text{O}_2(g) \longrightarrow 2\text{Na}_2\text{O}(s) $$
    • iii) **magnesium and hydrochloric acid:** Magnesium reacts with hydrochloric acid to form magnesium chloride and hydrogen gas. $$ \text{Mg}(s) + 2\text{HCl}(aq) \longrightarrow \text{MgCl}_2(aq) + \text{H}_2(g) $$
  5. An element reacts with oxygen to give a compound with a high melting point. This compound is also soluble in water. Which element - calcium or carbon is it likely to be?

    It is likely to be **Calcium** ($\text{Ca}$).

    • **Reaction:** Calcium reacts with oxygen to form **Calcium Oxide** ($\text{CaO}$), which is an ionic compound with a high melting point.
    • $$\text{Ca} + \frac{1}{2}\text{O}_2 \longrightarrow \text{CaO}$$
    • **Solubility:** Calcium oxide is soluble in water, forming calcium hydroxide, a base.
    • $$\text{CaO} + \text{H}_2\text{O} \longrightarrow \text{Ca}(\text{OH})_2$$
    • In contrast, Carbon reacts with oxygen to form Carbon Dioxide ($\text{CO}_2$), which is a gas (not a solid with a high melting point) and is only slightly soluble in water.
  6. There are three materials $\text{A}$, $\text{B}$ and $\text{C}$. Materials $\text{A}$ and $\text{C}$ are soft and $\text{B}$ is lustrous and hard; but material $\text{B}$ is dull and not very hard. Identify the metals and non-metals from $\text{A}$, $\text{B}$ and $\text{C}$.

    The prompt contains a slight contradiction in the description of B (lustrous and hard, but dull and not very hard). Assuming the properties are intended to represent a clear distinction:

    • **A and C are soft:** Soft materials are generally **non-metals** (like Sulphur, Phosphorus) or very soft metals (like Sodium, Potassium). Given the limited choices, it's safer to identify them as non-metals first.
    • **B is lustrous and hard:** **Metals** are typically lustrous and hard. Even if a metal is "not very hard," it is still generally harder than the soft non-metals.
    • **Identification based on typical properties:**
      • **Metal:** **Material $\text{B}$** (Lustrous and Hard)
      • **Non-metals:** **Materials $\text{A}$ and $\text{C}$** (Soft)
  7. Graphite and iodine are non-metals but they shine. Explain it with suitable reasons.

    The property of shining (lustre) is generally associated with metals, but Graphite and Iodine are exceptions:

    • **Iodine:** Solid iodine crystals have a molecular lattice structure that allows for the reflection of light, giving them a violet-black, metallic sheen.
    • **Graphite:** It has a unique layered structure. The layers are held by weak forces, but within each layer, carbon atoms are strongly bonded. The presence of **delocalised electrons** in the layers (similar to metals, but only in the plane of the layers) allows it to absorb and re-emit light, giving it a black, shiny appearance.
  8. Compare between metals and non-metals on the basis of their chemical properties.
    Chemical Property Metals Non-metals
    1. Reactivity with Oxygen Form **Basic Oxides** (e.g., $\text{Na}_2\text{O}$). These dissolve in water to form bases ($\text{NaOH}$). Form **Acidic Oxides** (e.g., $\text{SO}_2$). These dissolve in water to form acids ($\text{H}_2\text{SO}_3$). Some form neutral oxides ($\text{CO}$).
    2. Reactivity with Water Many react with cold or hot water/steam to produce a metal hydroxide/oxide and hydrogen gas ($\text{H}_2$). Generally do not react with water.
    3. Ion Formation Tend to **lose electrons** to form positive ions (**cations**) (e.g., $\text{Na} \rightarrow \text{Na}^+ + \text{e}^-$). Tend to **gain electrons** to form negative ions (**anions**) (e.g., $\text{Cl} + \text{e}^- \rightarrow \text{Cl}^-$).
    4. Reactivity with Acids Generally react with dilute acids to produce a salt and hydrogen gas ($\text{H}_2$). Do not react with dilute non-oxidizing acids.
  9. Write any three points to show the importance of each: i) zinc ii) sodium iii) potassium
    1. Zinc ($\text{Zn}$):
      • **Galvanisation:** Used to coat iron to prevent rusting.
      • **Alloys:** Used to make important alloys like Brass (Copper + Zinc).
      • **Biology:** Essential trace element for human health, involved in immune function and wound healing.
    2. Sodium ($\text{Na}$):
      • **Common Salt:** Sodium Chloride ($\text{NaCl}$) is essential for the human diet.
      • **Nerve Impulses:** Sodium ions ($\text{Na}^+$) are crucial for the transmission of nerve signals and muscle contraction (Sodium-Potassium pump).
      • **Industry:** Used in the production of caustic soda ($\text{NaOH}$) and for extraction of metals like titanium.
    3. Potassium ($\text{K}$):
      • **Nerve Impulses:** Potassium ions ($\text{K}^+$) work with Sodium ions for the proper function of nerve and muscle cells.
      • **Fertilisers:** Used extensively in the form of compounds (Potash) as an essential plant nutrient in agricultural fertilisers.
      • **Regulating Water Balance:** Helps to regulate the body's fluid balance and blood pressure.
  10. Mercury and lead are harmful metals for human beings. How are these metals harmful? Suggest your views.
    • **Harmfulness:** Both Mercury ($\text{Hg}$) and Lead ($\text{Pb}$) are **heavy metals** and highly toxic. They are non-biodegradable and accumulate in the body over time (bioaccumulation), causing long-term, irreversible damage.
    • **Mercury:** Affects the **nervous system** (leading to neurological disorders like Minamata disease), kidneys, and is especially harmful to developing fetuses.
    • **Lead:** Affects the **nervous system** (especially in children, causing learning disabilities), and interferes with **blood formation** and kidney function.
    • **Suggested Views (Measures to Reduce Harm):**
      1. **Regulation:** Strict government control and complete ban on the use of lead in petrol (already done in many places), paints, and toys.
      2. **Disposal:** Proper and safe disposal/recycling of products containing mercury (e.g., old thermometers, fluorescent bulbs) to prevent environmental contamination.
      3. **Monitoring:** Regular monitoring of drinking water, soil, and air quality for safe levels of these heavy metals, especially near industrial areas.

  14. If you are given two samples, how do you differentiate which one is metal and which one is non-metal?

    The best way is to test their **physical properties**, as chemical tests can be dangerous or slow. Three key tests are recommended:

    1. **Lustre Test:** Check the surface appearance. The **metal** will be **shiny** (lustrous), and the **non-metal** will be **dull**.
    2. **Malleability/Brittleness Test (Hammer Test):** Hit the samples gently with a hammer. The **metal** will flatten out (malleable), and the **non-metal** will break into pieces (brittle).
    3. **Conductivity Test:** Use the electrical circuit set-up (Question 6). The **metal** will make the bulb **glow** (conductor), and the **non-metal** will not (insulator).
